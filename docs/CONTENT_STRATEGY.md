# Content Strategy

Version 1.0 editorial foundation for the architecture studio website.

## Purpose

The website should communicate the studio through atmosphere, restraint, project quality, and editorial precision.

The content should not behave like agency marketing. It should feel closer to an architecture monograph: measured, quiet, visual, and confident.

The role of writing is to support the work, not overpower it.

## Studio Philosophy

The studio designs architecture and interiors through a close reading of place, material, light, and daily ritual.

The work is guided by restraint. Each project should feel composed rather than decorated, calm rather than empty, and precise rather than minimal for its own sake.

Architecture is treated as a long-term relationship between context and atmosphere. A successful space should feel inevitable over time: quietly useful, materially honest, and emotionally clear.

Core beliefs:

- Architecture should frame life rather than perform for attention.
- Light is a structural material.
- Restraint creates clarity.
- Material should be tactile, durable, and purposeful.
- The best spaces gather meaning slowly.
- Context matters more than visual novelty.

## Positioning

The studio is positioned as a premium architecture and interior design practice for clients who value calm, detail, longevity, and atmosphere.

It should not sound like a luxury lifestyle brand. It should sound like a disciplined architectural practice with strong taste and a clear point of view.

Positioning statement:

```text
An architecture and interiors studio shaping calm, enduring spaces through light, material, proportion, and context.
```

What the studio is:

- Editorial
- Architecture-first
- Material-led
- Quietly premium
- Contextual
- Detail-oriented
- Timeless

What the studio is not:

- Trend-led
- Loud luxury
- Generic design agency
- Real estate marketing
- Lifestyle decoration
- Fast visual spectacle

## Audience

Primary audiences:

- Private residential clients seeking architecture or interiors with restraint and longevity.
- Boutique hospitality founders who value atmosphere over trend.
- Developers commissioning premium small-scale residential, cultural, or hospitality work.
- Cultural and gallery clients who need refined spatial experiences.
- Design-conscious collaborators, makers, photographers, and consultants.

Audience mindset:

- They are visually literate.
- They respond to silence, detail, and material quality.
- They do not need aggressive selling.
- They want trust, taste, and evidence of judgment.
- They care about how a space feels as much as how it looks.

## Tone Of Voice

The voice should be calm, precise, and editorial.

Use:

- Short, confident sentences.
- Specific architectural language.
- Material, light, atmosphere, threshold, proportion, and context.
- Subtle emotional language when it is grounded in space.
- Concrete project details.

Avoid:

- Hype.
- Sales urgency.
- Generic luxury wording.
- Excessive adjectives.
- Empty claims.
- Trend language.

Preferred tone:

```text
A courtyard residence shaped by stone, shade, and open thresholds near the Aegean coast.
```

Avoid:

```text
A stunning luxury villa designed to deliver an unforgettable lifestyle experience.
```

## Editorial Writing Style

Project writing should read like a compact architecture publication.

Rules:

- Start with the architectural idea, not the client benefit.
- Mention site, light, material, or spatial organization early.
- Keep excerpts to one sentence.
- Keep descriptions clear and grounded.
- Avoid explaining every feature.
- Let images carry much of the emotion.

Sentence rhythm:

- Mix short sentences with one longer editorial sentence.
- Avoid long stacked abstractions.
- Use commas sparingly.
- Let key words breathe.

Preferred vocabulary:

- atmosphere
- restraint
- threshold
- proportion
- material
- light
- shade
- courtyard
- stone
- plaster
- timber
- quiet
- measured
- enduring
- tactile
- context
- sequence

Avoid vocabulary:

- stunning
- luxurious
- world-class
- cutting-edge
- innovative solution
- dream home
- elevated lifestyle
- unforgettable experience
- bespoke elegance
- premium living

## Project Categories

Use a restrained category taxonomy.

Version 1.0 categories:

- Residential
- Interior
- Architecture
- Hospitality

Future optional categories:

- Cultural
- Gallery
- Landscape
- Workplace

Rules:

- Each project should have one primary category.
- Avoid overly granular categories.
- Do not use marketing labels such as Luxury, Premium, Modern, or Exclusive.
- Use category names consistently across CMS, archive, SEO, and structured data.

## Naming Conventions

Project names should be short, architectural, and memorable.

Name sources:

- Place
- Material
- Light condition
- Spatial type
- Landscape condition
- Architectural idea

Good patterns:

- Courtyard Residence
- Stone Apartment
- North Light Gallery
- Limestone Hotel
- Ridge House
- Oak Room
- Plaster House
- Garden Pavilion

Avoid:

- The Ultimate Villa
- Luxury Dream Residence
- Modern Living Concept
- Premium Interior Design Project

Rules:

- Use title case.
- Keep names between two and four words.
- Avoid client names unless public and appropriate.
- Avoid city names unless place is central to the identity.
- Avoid abstract poetic titles that do not describe the work.

## Photography Direction

Photography is the primary content system.

Images should feel architectural before they feel decorative.

Preferred:

- Wide compositions.
- Natural daylight.
- Soft shadows.
- Long perspectives.
- Clear material surfaces.
- Calm human traces.
- Spatial depth.
- Negative space.
- Architectural thresholds.

Avoid:

- Over-processed HDR.
- Strong orange sunsets.
- Blue-hour real estate photography.
- Over-furnished rooms.
- Lifestyle posing.
- Cropped decorative details as primary images.
- Images where furniture competes with architecture.

Cover image rules:

- Must work at full-width desktop scale.
- Must retain clarity on mobile.
- Should show spatial depth.
- Should not be too dark.
- Should avoid busy textures behind text-heavy areas.

Gallery rules:

- Sequence images like a walkthrough.
- Start wide, then move into threshold, material, light, and detail.
- Avoid repeating the same angle.
- Include captions only when they add meaning.

## SEO Voice

SEO should be clear and descriptive without becoming generic.

Global SEO voice:

- calm
- premium
- architecture-focused
- location-aware where useful
- project-specific

Title format:

```text
Page or Project Title | Studio Name
```

Project SEO description format:

```text
Project type and location, described through material, light, atmosphere, and architectural intent.
```

Example:

```text
Courtyard Residence is a residential architecture project shaped by limestone, shade, and open thresholds near the Aegean coast.
```

Avoid:

- keyword stuffing
- repeated service names
- overpromising
- generic luxury phrases

Recommended keywords:

- architecture studio
- interior design studio
- residential architecture
- hospitality interiors
- architectural portfolio
- quiet luxury interiors
- material-led architecture
- contemporary architecture

## CMS Content Strategy

Payload should become the source of truth for project content first, then broader editorial content.

Current priority:

1. Projects
2. Project categories
3. Media
4. SEO metadata
5. Site settings
6. Contact information
7. Navigation

Future CMS expansion:

- Homepage content global
- Studio page content global
- Contact page content global
- Footer/social links global
- Services content
- Journal or essays
- Press and awards, only if needed

Project CMS rules:

- Every published project must have title, slug, category, year, location, excerpt, hero image, and published status.
- Featured projects must also be published.
- Gallery images should include alt text.
- Project excerpts should be editorial and concise.
- SEO fields should be completed before launch.
- Do not publish placeholder content unless clearly acceptable for demo mode.

Content status:

- Draft: incomplete or internal.
- Published: ready for public site.
- Featured: selected for homepage emphasis.

Editorial review checklist:

- Does the title feel architectural?
- Is the excerpt specific?
- Is the description calm and useful?
- Are materials described with purpose?
- Is the location formatted consistently?
- Does the cover image work on desktop and mobile?
- Does SEO describe the project without hype?

## Governance

Every content update should preserve the same tone and taxonomy.

Before adding a new category, service, or page type, confirm that it cannot be handled by the existing system.

The project should grow like a publication, not like a feature checklist.
